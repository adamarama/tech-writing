# How to Assemble a Bicycle: A Step-by-Step Guide

## Overview
When ordered online, bicycles typically arrive disassembled to save on shipping costs. Fortunately, putting your bike together at home is a relatively simple process. This guide will walk you through assembling a new bicycle step-by-step and include quality checks so you can be sure the finished product will be safe and ready for the road.

> **You will need:**
> - Allen (hex) wrench set
> - Adjustable wrench
> - Bike grease
> - Tire pump

<img src="/images/002.png" alt text="A set of Allen (hex) wrenches | Source: Dave Caudrey / Immediate Media"  style="width: 500px;">

> **Optional but recommended:**
> - Torque wrench
> - Bike stand

## Before You Begin
- **Start by reviewing the owner's manual.** This will contain important information about your specific bicycle model.
	- If you don't have the owner's manual, try searching for your bike's model online; it may be possible to download a digital version.
	- Look into local bike shops if you need more help or have additional questions.
- **Verify that you have all the necessary parts.** Ensure all components and hardware (bolts, nuts, washers) are included.
  - If something is missing, notify the seller for more help.
  - Sometimes, small parts can get stuck in the protective packaging, so you should wait to throw anything away until it is finished.
- **Prepare your workspace.** Find a clean, flat space with plenty of room.

## Step 1: Attach the Front Fork to the Frame

1. **Get the bike frame into the correct position.** Start by placing the frame on the bike stand or having someone hold it upright.
2. **Attach the front fork.** Insert the front fork component's steerer tube into the frame's head tube.
3. **Install the stem.** Slide the stem and any spacers onto the steerer tube.
4. **Tighten the stem bolts.** Use an Allen (hex) wrench to secure the fork.

> **Quality Check:**
> - Check that the fork turns smoothly without resistance or wobbling.
> - Confirm that the fork correctly aligns with the frame and the rear wheel.

## Step 2: Install the Seat

<img src="/images/010.png" alt text="Checking the seat height | Source: Dave Caudrey / Immediate Media"  style="width: 500px;">

1. **Locate the minimum insertion mark.** Inspect the seat post and find the mark that indicates how far you must insert the post into the seat tube to be secure. You may adjust the seat higher or lower as desired, but this mark indicates the maximum safe height.
	- A seat post not inserted deeply enough into the seat tube may damage or destroy the frame if the bike is ridden.
	- If you desire a higher seat than the minimum insertion mark allows, you must purchase an alternate component separately. 
2. **Apply a small amount of bike grease to the seat post.** This will reduce friction between the bicycle components and prevent seizing.
3. **Install the seat post.** Insert the seat post into the tube and push it in until the minimum insertion mark is inside the seat tube. Adjust it to the preferred height.
4. **Tighten the seat post clamp.** Use a wrench or a quick-release mechanism, depending on the bike model. Secure the seat post so it isn't jarred out of place.

> **Quality Check:**
> - Ensure the seat is level and aligned with the bike frame.
> - Confirm that the seat post is secure and does not slip when applying pressure.

## Step 3: Install the Handlebars

<img src="/images/004.png" alt text="Checking the handlebars | Source: Dave Caudrey / Immediate Media"  style="width: 500px;">

1. **Correctly position the fork.** Face the fork forward and align the handlebars with the stem so the brake levers are correctly positioned (usually slightly angled downward or parallel to the ground).
2. **Secure and immobilize the handlebars.** Tighten the stem bolts using an Allen (hex) wrench.
3. **Inspect the brake cables.** Make sure they haven't gotten tangled up or knotted.

> **Quality Check:**
> - Double-check that the handlebars are centered and secure.
> - Confirm that the brakes engage smoothly by squeezing the levers.
> - Ensure the brake cables are free from twists or kinks.

## Step 4: Install the Front Wheel

<img src="/images/007.png" alt text="Inflating the front wheel | Source: Dave Caudrey / Immediate Media"  style="width: 500px;">

1. **Inspect the tires.** By going over each side of the wheel, Make sure that each tire is correctly mounted on its rim. Confirm that the tires are evenly placed over the rims and adjust if necessary.
2. **Find the recommended air pressure.** This typically appears on the tire itself, but you can also refer to the owner's manual or check online if you need more help.
3. **Inflate the tires.** Remove the caps and use the tire pump to inflate the tires until you achieve the recommended air pressure. Be sure to avoid accidentally bursting your tires by inflating them slowly.
4. **Recheck the tires.** After spinning the wheels, check that the tires are still seated evenly on the rims. If not, release the air and repeat the process until they are properly positioned. When you are finished, replace the valve cap.
5. **Attach the front wheel.** Open the front brake calipers so the wheel slides into the fork, then place the wheel into the front fork dropouts.
6. **Tighten the bolts.** Tighten each bolt a little at a time, move on to the others and tighten them just as much, repeating until you have fully tightened all the bolts. Double-check that the wheel is still centered between the fork.
7. **Close the front brake calipers.** Adjust the brake pads if necessary.

> **Quality Check:**
> - Spin the front wheel to ensure it rotates freely without rubbing against the brake pads.
> - Make sure the bolts are tight but not over-tightened.

## Step 5: Attach the Pedals

<img src="/images/009.png" alt text="Tightening the pedals | Source: Dave Caudrey / Immediate Media"  style="width: 500px;">

1. **Identify the left and right pedals.** Find which is which by checking the spindles and finding the "L" and "R" markers. 
2. **Apply a little bike grease to the pedal threads.** This will make removing them easier in the future.
3. **Attach the right pedal.** Thread the right pedal clockwise into the right crank arm and hand-tighten.
4. **Attach the left pedal.** Thread the left pedal counterclockwise into the left crank arm and hand-tighten.
5. **Tighten the pedals.** Use an adjustable wrench to tighten both pedals securely.

> **Quality Check:**
> - Confirm that both pedals are secured.
> - Spin the pedals to ensure they rotate smoothly.

## Step 6: Final Adjustments and Inspection

<img src="/images/005.png" alt text="Checking the wheel | Source: Dave Caudrey / Immediate Media"  style="width: 500px;">

1. **Recheck all hardware.** Double-check the tightness of all bolts, nuts, and quick-release mechanisms.
2. **Check the brakes.** Ensure the brakes are functioning correctly and adjust them if needed. When engaged, the brake pads should not rub on the tire itself. Instead, the pads should rest on and make contact with the rim.
3. **Test the gears.** If your bike has gears, raise the rear tire off the ground and test the gear shifting mechanism to ensure smooth transitions between gears.
4. **Adjust the levers.** Any gear or brake levers should be in front of the handlebar. Squeeze them to make sure they are functioning correctly.
5. **Check the reflectors.** Make sure the angles of both the front and back reflectors are correct when the bicycle is on level ground.

> **Quality Check:**
> - Double-check that you don't have any parts or hardware left over, indicating that you missed something along the way.
> - Perform a final check to ensure everything is secure, tightened, and aligned.
> - Take your bike for a short test ride to check for any issues or unusual noises and adjust as needed.

With your bicycle fully assembled, you're ready to hit the road! However, be sure to inspect it regularly before each ride to ensure it remains in top condition.
